Collective-cognitive resources that could be used to discuss real systemic dynamics that are not only way more relevant for the entities than those ramified details but actually detrimental for their own self-preservation.

The result is a considerable epistemic exhaustion: many can no longer distinguish established facts from fiction because the line between them has been deliberately blurred.

It consumes people’s epistemic time and attention.

When public opinion is busy chasing phantoms it stops scrutinizing: drain wealth and systemic opression, injustice, inequality; other forms of exploitation that do exist but not in the way the conspiracy describes; monopoly concentration, algorithmic control that shape what people see and how they interact.

Narratives serve to discredit legitimate investigations. When real accusations of corruption or abuse surface, the powerful can label everything as “conspiracy theory” because the field has already been contaminated with absurd stories. This is known as poisoning the well, one of the main Informal fallacies. 

A great number of informal fallacies have been identified, including the fallacy of equivocation, the fallacy of amphiboly, the fallacies of composition and division, the false dilemma, the fallacy of begging the question, the ad hominem fallacy and the appeal to ignorance. 
One approach sometimes found in the literature is to distinguish between fallacies of ambiguity, which have their root in ambiguous or vague language, fallacies of presumption, which involve false or unjustified premises, and fallacies of relevance, in which the premises are not relevant to the conclusion despite appearances otherwise.
